I. Unsharp Masking Sharpen

Although it may seem counterintuitive, unsharp masking is actually a technique used to sharpen images. Unsharp masking is the technique of adding (some multiple alpha of) the high frequencies of an image to the original image, thus accentuating the high frequency components of the image. The high frequencies of the image, also called the Laplacian, is found by subtracting the low frequencies, which is found by smoothing the original image with a Gaussian filter, away from the original image.

II. Hybrid Image

Hybrid images can be created by combining the high frequencies of one with the the low frequencies of another image. This can be accomplished because it's more difficult to distinguish high frequency feature from further away than low frequency features and vice versa. This means that if we are able to composite the high frequencies of one image onto the low frequencies of another image, we will create a hybrid image that looks like one thing from afar, and something else up close!

Although it will be difficult to make out the details of the low frequency image from up close, it will still interfere with our view of the high frequency image. If the images are similar and aligned properly, this interferance is minimalized. I chose to use the images of a cake and a pie, since they're both vaguely ellipical.

I applied a low-pass filter, which is a standard 2D Gaussian filter (the same as the one from the previous section) of sigma 8, to the cake, and a high-pass filter, which is the Laplacian (the Laplacian of the image is the original subtracted by the Gaussian-filtered image, also used in the previous section) of sigma 3, to the pie. These sigma values are chosen through guessing and checking.

Beside these images are their the 2D Fourier transforms, which we can use to observe the effects of these filters and combinations. The pie's 2D Fourier transforms has rotated somewhat. Also, note how in the cake's 2D Fourier transforms how the low frequency values (white reprsents high frequency, and black represents low frequency) are eliminated.

Woo! You can now look at both a cake and pie at the same time. This is what I call late night food blog browsing efficiency. But also look at the 2D Fourier transforms as well. We can see see both the low-pass and high-pass frequency images' features in it. Pretty neat!

III. Gaussian and Laplacian Stacks

Gaussian and Lacplacian stacks can be very useful when you need multiple Gaussians or Laplacians of a certain image with different sigmas, or frequency ranges. I chose to incrememnt sigma by a power of two between each level in the stack. (i.e. Level 1 has a sigma of 1, level 2 has a sigma of 2, level 3 has a sigma of 4, and so on.) Each level in the Gaussian is created simply by using the Gaussian filter on the image with the corresponding sigma. Each level in the Laplacian is created by subtracting the same level of the Gaussian stack by the level below it. (i.e. Laplacian4 = Gaussian3 - Gaussian4)

Gaussian and Laplacian stacks can also be used to evalutate hybrid images, like the ones created from the previous part. The Gaussian takes only the low frequencies of an image, so the further up the Gaussian stack we go, the clearer the low frequency part of the image we will see. The Laplacian takes the high frequencies of an image, so the further up the Laplacian stack we go, the clearer the high frequency part of the image we will see.

IV. Multiresolution Blending

Multiresoltion blending is the process of blending together two images seamlessly by computing its seam through separate high and low frequency calculations.

The Gaussian and Laplacian stacks from the previous section will be used to compute stacks for the two images we want to merge. Another Gaussian stack also needs to be created of the image mask. The process is to sum over the each level in the Laplacian stack multiplied by the corresponding level in the mask Gaussian stack for each Laplacian stack. This will produce Laplacians for each image masked by our image mask. which is also a seamless combination of the images' high frequencies. The last step is to add the highest level Gaussian of the mask stack multiplied by the original images to to get a seamless combination of the images' low frequencies. This process is illustrated in the example below.

I chose images of dogs from dogtime.com, because the dogs in these images are in the same pose, and have white backgrounds. I manually aligned the two images and created a vertical mask in Photoshop. I ended up using stack size 4, just because it seemed the most seamless to me.
